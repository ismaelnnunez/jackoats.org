Finance

Financial Instruments are instruments that have a monetary value, and represent a liability to one party and an asset to another.[2] An instrument is a legal document that records agreements or actions that are legally enforceable.

Examples of legal instruments include contracts, wills, deeds, and statutes.[3]

Examples of financial instruments include stocks, bonds, options contracts, Certificates of Deposit (CDs). Another example of a type of financial instrument is a security. Securities are fungible, negotiable financial instruments.

Fungibility is the ability for something to be interchanged for something else of the same type for the same value. Currency, for example, is fungible because a $1 bill is always going to be worth $1 no matter who owns it or where you use it. Real estate is a non-fungible legal instrument because no one property is identical to another and its value is dependent on several factors like location, history, flood exposure, etc. [4]

Further, securities represent ownership in some sort of asset (in something that is worth some monetary value).

Index Funds vs. Exchange-Traded-Funds vs. Mutual Funds

An Index is not traded, it is just a list of stocks. The S&P 500 is an example of an index, as is the NASDAQ. An index fund is an umbrella term for many exchange-traded-funds (ETFs) and mutual funds. If a fund – a pool of money that is invested – tracks an index, it is an index fund.

Mutual funds are created by companies and are typically not traded in a stock exchange. You can trade shares of these funds usually directly with the company, such as Fidelity or Vanguard. FXAIX is an example of a mutual fund. Mutual fund prices are usually posted at the end of the trading day.

ETFs are like mutual funds, except that they are traded in public stock exchanges and are also therefore posted variably throughout the day like any individual stock.

Order Types

An order

is quite simply that - an order to trade (buy or sell) a certain stock. But there are several types of orders you can make thanks to the power of automation[5]:
  • Market Order - These are the most straightforward types of orders. Market orders are orders to buy or sell immediately at the current price. A posted ask is the current price to buy, and the posted bid is the current price to sell. Market orders are good if you want to guarantee that you get the current price and do not want to subject your order to volatility.
  • Limit Order - These orders allow you to place an order if the stock reaches a desired price. Different types of limit orders specify exactly what this threshold is and what criteria are attached to it:
    • Buy limit orders specify the maximum price at which to buy a stock. If the price of a stock falls below your limit, the order will be executed. This also means any price up to your desired maximum is fair game. The price of a stock might go down rapidly, “never reaching” the desired limit. There also might not be enough buyers at a certain price, further driving the cost down below the desired limit.
    • Sell limit orders function the same way but the other way around: a sell limit order specifies the minimum price at which to execute a sell.
